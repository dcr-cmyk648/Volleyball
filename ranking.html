Summary

This app uses a modern rating system specifically designed for ranking players who play in frequently-changing teams. The basic idea is that every player has both an estimated skill level and a level of uncertainty. Julia Ibstedt, Elsa Rådahl, Erik Turesson, and Magdalena vande Voorde. Application and further development of trueskill™ ranking in sports. 2019. Ruby C. Weng and Chih-Jen Lin. A bayesian approximation method for online ranking. Journal of Machine Learning Research, 12(9):267–300, 2011.

Mu = estimated skill
Sigma = uncertainty about that estimate
Public Ranking = mu - 3 × sigma

A player rises when they perform well against strong opposition and falls when results are worse than expected. The system is also more cautious when it has less data and more confident when it has seen many games.

The basic idea

Instead of saying “this player is exactly a 1542,” the model says something closer to:

“Based on the games recorded so far, this player appears to be around this skill level, and this is how certain we are about that estimate.”

That is what mu and sigma are doing.

  • Higher mu means the model thinks the player is stronger.
  • Higher sigma means the model is less certain.
  • Lower sigma means the model has more confidence.

The app then publishes a conservative ranking based on mu - 3 × sigma. That means the public ranking does not only reward high estimated skill. It also rewards having enough data for that estimate to be trusted.

Why confidence matters

Early on, the system is unsure about everyone. A new player starts with a reasonable default estimate but also a fairly wide confidence interval. After only a few games, the model may suspect that someone is strong, but it does not immediately treat that estimate as settled fact.

As more games are recorded, the model has more evidence. Sigma gradually falls, which means confidence rises. That is why players with a longer track record tend to have more stable rankings, while newer players can move more quickly.

In plain English: the app becomes less gullible as it sees more data.

How team games are handled

The app rates volleyball as a team game, not as a collection of isolated one-on-one matchups. A game result updates all players who participated, but the update depends on the strength of the teams involved, not just the final winner.

This lets the model handle:

  • changing teams from game to game
  • uneven player experience levels
  • the fact that one player’s result is partly shaped by teammates and opponents

How does score margin affect ranking?

I didn't think that a narrow win such as 26–24 should not be treated exactly the same as a lopsided result such as 25–10. At the same time, blowouts should not completely dominate the rankings. So the app uses a bounded margin adjustment: larger score differences matter more, but only up to a cap.

In practical terms, that means score margin can influence the ranking change after a game by around 20-30%

Why is my ranking low when I have won most of my games?

A player can have a good estimated skill but still have a middling public ranking if the model is not yet very certain. This is a feature, not a bug. It helps prevent someone from jumping unrealistically high after only a few strong appearances.

It also means players with more sustained results (who play more games) usually separate more clearly over time.

What “Seasonal ranking (beta)” means

I added this mode to support future mini-leagues where players can be ranked based primarily on their performance within a specific window, rather than relying heavily on historical performance.

Older games are not deleted, but their effect is tapered down. Very recent games count most. Games from roughly three months ago or older contribute much less.
