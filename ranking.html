How Ranking Works

This is a Stats reference page. Use the Stats tab to return to standings and history.

Summary

This app uses a modern rating system specifically designed for ranking players who play in frequently-changing teams. The basic idea is that every player has both an estimated skill level and a level of uncertainty.

References
  • Julia Ibstedt, Elsa Rådahl, Erik Turesson, and Magdalena vande Voorde. Application and further development of trueskill™ ranking in sports. 2019.
  • Ruby C. Weng and Chih-Jen Lin. A bayesian approximation method for online ranking. Journal of Machine Learning Research, 12(9):267–300, 2011.
Mu
estimated skill
Sigma
uncertainty about that estimate
Public Ranking is a linear transform that maps the adjusted raw ordinal range onto readable 4-digit scores.

A player rises when they perform well against strong opposition and falls when results are worse than expected. The system is also more cautious when it has less data and more confident when it has seen many games.

1

Why confidence matters

Early on, the system is unsure about everyone. A new player starts with a reasonable default estimate but also a fairly wide confidence interval. After only a few games, the model may suspect that someone is strong, but it acknowledges that the captured games may randomly have been that player's stronger games.

As more games are recorded, the model has more evidence. Sigma gradually falls, which means confidence rises. That is why players with a longer track record tend to have more stable rankings, while newer players' rankings can move more erratically.

2

How team games are handled

A game result updates all players who participated, but the update depends on the strength (aggregate mu and sigma) of the teams involved, not just the final winner.

You get more points for winning games the model expected you to lose, based on the strength of your team and the opponent's team

3

How does score margin affect ranking?

Narrow and ordinary-margin wins are treated as normal wins. Clear blowouts can add a small capped bonus, but blowouts do not dominate the rankings.

In practical terms, a 25-10 result moves ratings slightly more than a 25-21 result, while a 26-24 result is treated like an ordinary win.

4

Why is my ranking low when I have won most of my games?

A player can have a good estimated skill but still have a slightly lower public ranking if they have only played a few games. Raw ordinal already includes uncertainty through sigma, and the public board adds a small capped low-game penalty so a short lucky streak does not jump too far ahead of longer track records.

It also means players with more sustained results (who play more games) usually separate more clearly over time.

5

What does the current model use?

The model has two related jobs: update player ratings after games, and estimate balanced teams before games.

For rating updates, it currently takes into account:

  • OpenSkill mu and sigma for every player, including uncertainty.
  • Team result: who won, who lost, and which players were on each side.
  • Opponent strength: beating a stronger team moves ratings more than beating a weaker team.
  • Score margin, using a very small capped bonus for clearer wins.
  • Recentness: older games still count, but rating-update impact tapers over roughly a 6-month window.
  • New-player burn-in, so early games can move uncertain players toward their level faster.
  • Calibration for low-game players, which reduces distortions from everyone starting at the same default rating.
  • League games, including synthetic league-opponent ratings by league context.
  • A league-game update multiplier, because league results have tested as useful but noisier than ordinary head-to-head games.

For team balancing, it currently takes into account:

  • Each player's backend raw rating, not the confidence-adjusted public scoreboard number.
  • The top player and second-best player on each team.
  • Team depth and team average.
  • The weakest player on each team, with the weak-link effect strongest when the teams are otherwise close.
  • Team size, including support for uneven teams.
  • League-team strength when building league matchups.
  • Modeled win probability and expected point margin.
  • Historical carry score is currently disabled in balancing, because testing favored not using it.

The public scoreboard also applies a small low-game penalty before mapping raw ordinal onto the public rating scale. That display adjustment does not feed back into team balancing or OpenSkill updates.
