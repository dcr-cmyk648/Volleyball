How Ranking Works

This is a Stats reference page. Use the Stats tab to return to standings and history.

Summary

This app uses a modern rating system specifically designed for ranking players who play in frequently-changing teams. The basic idea is that every player has both an estimated skill level and a level of uncertainty.

References
  • Julia Ibstedt, Elsa Rådahl, Erik Turesson, and Magdalena vande Voorde. Application and further development of trueskill™ ranking in sports. 2019.
  • Ruby C. Weng and Chih-Jen Lin. A bayesian approximation method for online ranking. Journal of Machine Learning Research, 12(9):267–300, 2011.
Mu
estimated skill
Sigma
uncertainty about that estimate
Public Ranking is just a mathematical transform that makes scores easier for humans to read (by making them 4 digit numbers normed around 1500) without changing ranking order in the database

A player rises when they perform well against strong opposition and falls when results are worse than expected. The system is also more cautious when it has less data and more confident when it has seen many games.

1

Why confidence matters

Early on, the system is unsure about everyone. A new player starts with a reasonable default estimate but also a fairly wide confidence interval. After only a few games, the model may suspect that someone is strong, but it acknowledges that the captured games may randomly have been that player's stronger games.

As more games are recorded, the model has more evidence. Sigma gradually falls, which means confidence rises. That is why players with a longer track record tend to have more stable rankings, while newer players' rankings can move more erratically.

2

How team games are handled

A game result updates all players who participated, but the update depends on the strength (aggregate mu and sigma) of the teams involved, not just the final winner.

You get more points for winning games the model expected you to lose, based on the strength of your team and the opponent's team

3

How does score margin affect ranking?

I didn't think that a narrow win such as 26–24 should be treated exactly the same as a lopsided result such as 25–10. At the same time, blowouts should not completely dominate the rankings. The app uses a bounded margin adjustment where larger score differences matter more, but only up to a cap.

In practical terms, that means score margin can influence the ranking change after a game by around 20-30%

4

Why is my ranking low when I have won most of my games?

A player can have a good estimated skill but still have a middling public ranking if the model is not yet very certain. This reduces (but doesn't eliminate) the likelihood that someoone will have an unrealistically high rank when they have only played a few games.

It also means players with more sustained results (who play more games) usually separate more clearly over time.

5

What are the checkboxes/sliders/other inteactive elements?

These are mathematical adjustments for applying team skill logic more specifically to volleyball and seasonal play.

I added the seasonal mode to support future mini-leagues where players can be ranked based primarily on their performance within a specific window, rather than relying heavily on historical performance. Older games are not deleted, but their effect is tapered down. Very recent games count most (near-100% effect if within "season"). Games outside of the season window have a rapidly scaling penalty applied to their positive (or negative) impact.

The volleyball-adjustment checkbox applies the team balance logic from the MAX algorithm to how openskill adjusts rankings after matches. The MAX algorithm has been shown to more consistently produce accurate results for team-based games where average skill is variable.

League team incorporation gives more data but add a lot of noise because the performance of other teams in the league is so variable. Still fun to see.
